The Norse Wind Turbine Project, a first-year
Mechanical Engineering student project, showcasing
a comprehensive study on the design and
implementation of a wind turbine farm in the
Orkland Islands, northwest Scotland. This project
integrates both mechanical design and
computational simulation to optimize the placement
and functionality of wind turbines while
considering environmental and social factors such
as local wildlife and community acceptance.
I utilised Autodesk Fusion 360 to explore various
wind turbine designs, focusing primarily on the
Horizontal Axis Wind Turbine. Different
configurations were also analysed, including
Vertical Axis Wind Turbines and dual HAWTs, but
settled on the traditional HAWT for its efficiency
and cost-effectiveness. Through Pugh’s matrix and
pairwise comparison tables, I prioritiSed the
components and design factors, concluding that a
standard gearbox system within the nacelle was
optimal due to its lower cost and
manufacturability. The CAD drawings produced in
this phase provided a detailed visual
representation of the proposed nacelle design.
The coding portion of the project involved using
MATLAB and the Floris function to simulate the
wind farm's operational dynamics. I validated the
Floris function’s accuracy with normalised
velocity and power output graphs, ensuring the
computational model's reliability. The scripts
generated visualisations for turbine placement,
wind speed impacts, and monthly variations in
power output, offering insights into the best
operational periods and the effects of wind
direction on efficiency.
The findings emphasize the importance of detailed
pre-implementation studies in renewable energy
projects to maximize efficiency while minimizing
environmental disruptions. This project not only
enhanced my skills in mechanical design and coding
but also highlighted the interdisciplinary nature
of engineering projects that require both
computational and creative problem-solving
capabilities.
